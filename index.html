Gilded Age

A period in the late 19th century (1870s-1900) marked by rapid industrialization, economic growth, and immense wealth for some, but also significant social inequalities and political corruption.

This era saw the rise of powerful industrialists and monopolies, leading to a wealth gap between the rich and poor, alongside movements for reform and labor rights.

John D. Rockefeller (Standard Oil Company)

Rockefeller was the founder of Standard Oil, which became a massive monopoly in the oil industry, controlling nearly all oil refining in the U.S. by the late 1800s.

He became one of the richest men in history, and his companyâ€™s practices led to public outcry and the eventual breakup of Standard Oil under antitrust laws.

Andrew Carnegie (U.S. Steel Company)

Carnegie was a steel tycoon whose company, U.S. Steel, dominated the steel industry, making him one of the wealthiest men of his time.

Carnegie's dominance in the steel industry contributed to America's rapid industrial growth and his later philanthropic efforts shaped U.S. education and culture.

Monopoly

A monopoly is when a single company or group controls all or nearly all of a particular market or industry, limiting competition.

Monopolies were common during the Gilded Age, leading to public backlash and the eventual passage of antitrust laws to promote competition.

Vertical Integration and Horizontal Integration

Vertical integration is when a company controls all stages of production, while horizontal integration involves merging with or acquiring competitors in the same industry.

These strategies were used by tycoons like Carnegie and Rockefeller to dominate their industries and limit competition, contributing to the rise of monopolies.

American Federation of Labor

Founded in 1886, the AFL was a national federation of labor unions focused on improving wages, hours, and working conditions for skilled workers.

The AFL played a key role in advancing the labor movement in the U.S., securing better labor conditions for skilled workers during a time of widespread exploitation.

Samuel Gompers

Gompers was the founder and longtime leader of the American Federation of Labor, advocating for workers' rights through organized labor.

His leadership helped establish labor unions as a force for improving wages, hours, and working conditions in an industrializing America.

Ellis Island and Angel Island

Ellis Island (East Coast) and Angel Island (West Coast) were major immigration processing stations where millions of immigrants entered the U.S. in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.

These stations processed millions of immigrants, shaping the demographic and cultural makeup of the United States during the industrial era.

Nativism

Nativism is the policy of protecting the interests of native-born citizens against those of immigrants, often accompanied by anti-immigrant sentiment.

Nativist movements led to restrictions on immigration, including laws like the Chinese Exclusion Act, reflecting widespread fear of job competition and cultural change.

Chinese Exclusion Act

A U.S. federal law passed in 1882 that banned Chinese immigration and prevented Chinese immigrants already in the country from becoming citizens.

The act was a significant moment in U.S. immigration policy, marking the first time the country enacted a law to exclude a specific ethnic group from immigrating.
