Theodore Roosevelt

Theodore Roosevelt was the 26th President of the United States, known for his progressive policies, trust-busting, and conservation efforts, as well as his role in the building of the Panama Canal.

Roosevelt's presidency reshaped American politics, breaking up monopolies and initiating conservation programs that preserved natural resources for future generations.

Muckrakers

Muckrakers were investigative journalists in the early 20th century who exposed corruption and social issues, often spurring reforms during the Progressive Era.

Their work led to public awareness of corruption in politics and business, helping drive major reforms, such as improved labor laws and food safety regulations.

Upton Sinclair

Upton Sinclair was a muckraker and author of *The Jungle*, a novel that exposed the unsanitary and dangerous conditions in the American meatpacking industry, leading to food safety reforms.

Sinclair's book prompted the passage of the Meat Inspection Act and the Pure Food and Drug Act, ensuring safer food production practices in the U.S.

Ida Tarbell

Ida Tarbell was a pioneering journalist whose work exposed the unfair practices of the Standard Oil Company, helping to dismantle its monopoly.

Her investigative reporting contributed to the U.S. government's antitrust lawsuit, leading to the breakup of Standard Oil in 1911 and changes in corporate practices.

Plessy v. Ferguson

Plessy v. Ferguson was an 1896 Supreme Court case that upheld racial segregation under the "separate but equal" doctrine, which remained in effect until it was overturned in 1954.

The ruling institutionalized racial segregation in public facilities, contributing to the long-lasting era of Jim Crow laws that enforced discrimination against African Americans.
