Transcontinental Railroad

Completed in 1869, the Transcontinental Railroad connected the eastern U.S. rail network with the Pacific Coast, dramatically improving transportation and economic growth across the country.

The railroad made cross-country travel and shipping much faster, spurring settlement and economic development in the West, but also leading to displacement of Native Americans.

Invention of Barbed Wire

Patented in 1874, barbed wire revolutionized farming by allowing settlers to cheaply fence off large areas of land, leading to the end of the open range.

Barbed wire transformed ranching and farming practices in the American West, contributing to conflicts between farmers and cattle ranchers.

Destruction of Buffalo Population

The mass slaughter of buffalo in the late 19th century, primarily by settlers and hunters, drastically reduced the population, devastating Native American livelihoods that relied on the buffalo.

The near-extinction of the buffalo had a catastrophic impact on Plains Native American cultures, leading to starvation and loss of a critical resource.

Sitting Bull

Sitting Bull was a Hunkpapa Lakota leader who resisted U.S. government policies and played a key role in the Native American victory at the Battle of Little Bighorn.

Sitting Bull became a symbol of Native American resistance to U.S. expansion and the loss of their lands, ultimately leading to his death in 1890.

Ghost Dance

The Ghost Dance was a spiritual movement among Native Americans in the late 1800s, believed to bring peace and restore the lands and way of life lost to settlers.

The movement spread among Native American tribes but led to fear among U.S. authorities, culminating in the Massacre at Wounded Knee.

Battle of Little Bighorn

Fought in 1876, the Battle of Little Bighorn was a major victory for the Lakota and Cheyenne against U.S. forces, in which Lt. Col. Custer and his troops were defeated.

The battle was one of the last major victories for Native Americans in their fight to retain their land, but led to increased U.S. military pressure.

Massacre at Wounded Knee Creek

In 1890, U.S. soldiers killed hundreds of Lakota men, women, and children at Wounded Knee Creek, marking the tragic end of Native American armed resistance.

The massacre is often considered the end of Native American resistance to U.S. policies, symbolizing the final blow to the Plains tribes.
